# The Modular Architecture of Intelligence

A fundamental question in neuroscience is why are biological brains so insanely good at learning new things and generalizing to novel conditions? At least part of the answer seems to lie in a theory of compositionality. Here is what it means. Think about when you first learn to ride a bicycle. Your brain built a specific skill for handling two wheels. Let's call it a balancing module. Years later, you got your driver's license and in the process, you built a traffic rules module. Now suppose you want to learn to ride a motorcycle. To your brain, this is a new task because it has never encountered this specific behavior before. But you don't start from scratch. You take the balancing module from the bicycle, snap it together with the traffic rules module from the car, and boom, you're riding the motorcycle. The learning is incredibly quick.

The idea of compositionality states that the brain treats skills kind of like Lego bricks, building complex behaviors by snapping together reusable components. While this makes sense intuitively, how can we prove that this kind of mixing and matching happens physically at the level of individual neurons? And where in the brain is it happening? The authors looked at what happens in the brain of a monkey when it plays a special video game designed to probe exactly this idea. It consisted of three visual discrimination tasks. On every trial, the monkey sees a morphed image that has a certain shape on a spectrum between a bunny and a tea and color somewhere between red and green.

In the first task, the monkeys had to distinguish shapes. They had to move their eyes to the upper left corner of the screen if the image looked more like a bunny and to the lower right if it looked more like a T. If the monkey got the answer right, it received some juice as a reward. We'll call this task S1, which stands for shape axis one. In the second task, C2, the overall structure was similar, but the monkey had to report the color instead of shape and move its size along a different axis, too. If the image is more red than green, look top right. Otherwise, look bottom left. Finally, in the third task, C1, they had to report the color, but move their eyes along axis one. Look top left for green and bottom right for red. Do you see the beauty here? Task C1 is a composition. It takes the sensory rule from C2 look at color and the motor axis output from S1 move along the top left bottom right diagonal. If the brain is truly efficient, it should not build a new task C1 circuit completely from scratch. It should say, "Ah, I will just grab the color detector from C2 and wire it to the motor driver from S1." And this is exactly what the author set out to test to get a glimpse of what is happening in the brain.

They implanted electrodes in the prefrontal cortex, the area responsible for higher order abstract decisions, and recorded the activity of hundreds of individual neurons as the monkeys played the game. Because the building block modules involve more than one neuron, they couldn't just look at the activity of single cells in isolation. Instead, they needed a way to describe what is happening in the entire population of cells all at once. Imagine a coordinate system where every axis represents the activity level of a single neuron. If you record from a 100 neurons, you have a 100dimensional space. At any given millisecond, the state of the monkeykey's brain is a single point in that space. If we take brain activity from all the trials when the monkey saw the red category, we have a specific cloud of points and trials with green images form a different cloud.

Now there might be a specific direction in this neural activity space that best separates the two clouds. Mathematically this defines a decision boundary that slices the space into two halves, red and green. We'll call this direction a color subspace. We can use that separating hyper plane to build a classifier. It's a machine that takes a previously unseen data point in the activity space, checks which side of the boundary it falls on and hands you a prediction. It is a way to decode the identity of the color purely from neural activity. The accuracy of this classifier is then used to measure how much information there is in the brain about the color variable. The authors could successfully decode the color identity from cortical activity in both C1 and C2 tasks about half a second after the image was shown.

This means that this information was successfully processed and routed into the prefrontal cortex forming the two well-defined clouds in the activity space. Importantly, however, when the monkey was focusing on the shape in the S1 task, the color could not be decoded. This suggests that by the time image processing reaches the prefrontal cortex, this task irrelevant information is actively thrown away. The red and green clouds are all tangled up and squashed together. Similarly, the shape could be easily decoded during the S1 task, but not C1 or C2. Now, here is the critical test. If we think that building blocks are reused, it means that the color subspace should be the same in both tasks that rely on color processing. The hyper plane inside the classifier trained on task C2 should be capable of separating red and green clouds observed during task C1 and vice versa. The accuracy of this kind of cross task decoding should be high. And this is exactly what they found. When the researchers swap the decoders, they still worked almost perfectly.

In other words, the color module in the brain is physically reused between two different tasks. Similarly, a classifier trained to decode the movement direction generalized well between S1 and C1 tasks, suggesting that the motor command module is compositionally reused as well. So we have established that the brain has a color module and a motor module which exist as specific stable subspaces in neural activity. But having the parts isn't enough to actually solve the task. The brain has to connect them dynamically. In task C1, for example, the brain needs to take the red signal from the color subspace and route it to the move bottom right signal in the motor subspace. The researchers visualized this by tracking the neural activity within a single trial. About 100 milliseconds after the image appears, the neural state jumps into the color subspace.

But then a few hundred milliseconds later, the state flows out of the color subspace and rotates into the motor subspace to guide the eyes. Crucially, this flow depends on the context. In task C1, the red vector flows into axis one motor direction. In task C2, that same red vector flows into axis 2 motor direction. It acts exactly like a railroad switch. The cargo, the color is the same, but the task context flips a junction that routes the train down a different set of tracks to a different destination. However, this railroad switch only explains how we route the output. The brain also faces a second problem. Selecting the right input. Remember the monkey seen both shape and color. In task C1, it needs to route color and ignore shape. In task S1, it needs to route shape and ignore color. If the brain gets this one wrong, it sends the wrong cargo down the track. So, who decides which input to put on the train? To find out, the researchers exploited a helpful feature of the game, uncertainty. The monkeys performed tasks in blocks. When a new block started, the screen flashed, but the monkey wasn't told which task was next.

They only knew along which axis they had to move their eyes. If the motor axis was two, it was simple because only task C2 uses that axis. However, when the break ended and the monkeys had to perform a task along axis one, they faced a mystery. Is the rule color or shape? This created a perfect experimental window. For the first few dozen trials, the monkey had to guess, learn from feedback, and slowly update its internal belief about what the rule was. The researchers trained another decoder to read out this task belief from neural activity. They found that they could watch the monkeyy's realization in real time, watching the signal go from I think it's shape to, oh, it's definitely color. And here's the punch line. This abstract belief signal physically reconfigured the neural geometry. They found that task belief acts as a gain knob on sensory inputs. As the monkey realized he was in the color task, the brain amplified the color subspace, stretching the red and green clouds further apart. and simultaneously it suppressed the shape subspace squashing the bunny and T-clouds flat. This ensured that only the relevant information was loud enough to enter the routing switchboard.

So the mechanism of flexibility is twofold. The brain builds reusable orthogonal modules for every feature like color shape and motor command and then uses a central task belief signal to adjust the gain on the inputs and set the switches for the outputs. Putting all together, this paper provides evidence that the brain does not relearn the world for every new problem from scratch. Instead, it acts as an engineer building a library of fixed reusable components. When a new challenge arises, the brain simply has to engage relevant modules, compose them together to route information through the circuitry, and suppress irrelevant ones. This explains why humans can adapt to new environments so quickly. We're not building new neural hardware from scratch every time. Instead, we're just wiring up our old existing Lego bricks into a new configuration. If you enjoyed the video, share it with a friends.
